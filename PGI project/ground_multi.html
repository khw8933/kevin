  1. Propeller System

    • The propellers are constant speed, full feathering, and 2 blade (10‚Äù ground clearance)
    • Counter rotating prop system. Left turns clockwise, right turns counter clockwise (both engines are critical)
    • 2 forces are acting against each other: feathering forces and pitch controlling forces
    • Feathering forces: feathering springs, nitrogen charge, counterweight
    • Pitch controlling forces: speeder spring, flyweights, pilot valve
    • Propeller Governer moves oil back and forth through the propeller hub to make sure the prop is at the pitch and speed that you want
    • Flyweights are rotating with an engine. As a pilot move the prop lever, essentially the pilot is controlling the tension of the speeder spring. If a pilot moves the prop lever forward, the tension of the speeder spring will increase which makes flyweights fall inside and pilot valve moves down and the high pressure oil will enter the propeller hub to move the propeller into a fine pitch
    • If a pilot moves the prop lever backward, the tension of the speeder spring will decrease, which makes flyweights pull outside, and pilot valve moves up, and the oil in the propeller hub will draw out back to the sump
    • As RPM increases (overspeed), flyweights spin faster and due to the centrifugal force, flyweights will spin and pull outwards. Then pilot valve will move up, and the oil in the propeller hub will move out into the oil sump until the centrifugal force equals to the spring tension that the pilot set, thus, propeller moves to a coarse pitch
    • As RPM decreases (underspeed), flyweights spin less, and fall in towards the center, move the pilot valve down, letting the high pressure oil to enter the propeller hub and increase the RPM, thus, fine pitch
