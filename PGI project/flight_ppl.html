  1. Pre-flight inspection:

    • After the candidate has completed the pre-flight inspection, questions relating to the flight test aircraft will be asked.
      The candidate should be able to explain what appropriate action would be taken if an unsatisfactory item were detected
      or described by the examiner during the pre-flight inspection
    • Check compass card the day before and complete deviation section on the navigation log
    • Put a hood in the aircraft

  2. Departure & Enroute Procedures

    (i.e., ETA, 5T’s, RETA, position report,and ground speed check)

  3. Instrument flying & unusual attitude recovery

    • maintain co-coordinated straight and level flight for 2 minutes;
      carry out a level rate-one turn through 180° to a reciprocal compass heading and,
      on completion maintain co-coordinated straight and level flight for a further 2 minutes.
    • ±15º of the assigned heading; ±200 feet of the assigned altitude; ±15 knots of the assigned airspeed;

  4. Upper airwork:

    • Steep Turn
      • The candidate will be asked to execute a steep turn through 360º, with an angle of bank of 45º,
        using a pre-selected and prominent geographic point as a heading reference.
      • altitude (±100 feet), airspeed (±10 knots) and angle of bank (±10º)
        visually recover from the turn at the pre-selected recovery reference point (±10º)
    • Slow flight
      • demonstrate coordinated straight and level flight and a level turn, with an angle of bank of 15 degrees in slow flight
      • altitudes (±100 feet), headings (±10°) and angles of bank (±5°); roll out on specified headings (±10°)
    • Poweroff & Poweron Stall
      • recognize and announce the onset of the stall by identifying the stall warning, the first
      aerodynamic buffeting or decay of control effectiveness
    • Slipping
      • The candidate will be required to demonstrate a forward slip or a slipping turn to increase a rate of descent or a sideslip to correct for crosswind conditions during a landing
      • Note: Any significant skidding manoeuvre is unacceptable

  5. Lower airwork:

    • Forced approach
      • Engine failure will be simulated from 3,000 feet AGL without advance warning by the examiner by closing the throttle to idle or by bringing the power lever to flight idle and declaring “Simulated Engine Failure”
      • control the aeroplane and initially establish the recommended best glide speed (+10/-5 knots)
      • An approach that would have resulted in touching down more than 1,000 feet beyond the end of the selected touchdown zone in the first 1/3 of the landing area will be considered a major error and assessed with a mark of “2” or less
      • A change of field is acceptable from an altitude or point in the approach where a landing could still have been made on the originally chosen landing site
    • Precautionary landing
      • The examiner will outline a scenario requiring a landing. Scenarios may include a landing at an aerodrome of unknown condition or an off-field landing due to a deteriorating condition. The candidate must select a suitable landing area, determine the landing path, determine the appropriate precautionary procedure to be used and must use a planned procedure to fly an accurate approach. While an actual landing may not be required, the final approach flown should be such that a successful landing could have been accomplished in the pre-selected touchdown zone
    • Diversion
      • The candidate is expected to initiate the diversion without undue delay by quickly determining a track to follow, an approximate heading and an approximate time enroute to avoid the need to loiter in a holding pattern
      • The examiner will specify an initial altitude to fly. When practicable, a part or all of the diversion should be conducted at a height of between 500 and 1000 feet above ground or at the minimum safe altitude, whichever is higher. The candidate may change altitudes to suit the topography or the scenario and is expected to communicate intentional altitude changes to the examiner. Estimated enroute and arrival times may be approximate but with a reasonable degree of accuracy
      • The examiner will assess the candidate’s proficiency to proceed toward an alternate destination by using dead- reckoning or by following a series of geographic features such as roads, railways or rivers, where they are available

  6. Circuit:

    • landing briefing
      • types of landing
      • runway number
      • approach speed and flap setting
      • touch down point
    • Short field Takeoff and landing
    • Soft field takeoff and landing
    • Normal takeoff and landing

  7. Emergency:

    • Student will be asked 2 emergencies from one of the follwing items below either on the ground or in the air


    1. partial power loss
    2. rough engine operation or overheat
    3. loss of oil pressure
    4. fuel starvation
    5. electrical fire/smoke
    6. vacuum system failure
    7. pitot or static blockage
    8. cabin fire
    9. carb icing
    10. electrical malfunction
    11. landing gear malfunction
    12. brake failure or seizure
    13. flap failure
    14. door opening in flight
    15. spin recovery
    16. emergency descent
    17. any other emergency or malfunction unique to the aeroplane flown
